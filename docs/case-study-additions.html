ADDITION 2 / 5 · Decision 11 — paste after Decision 10.
Decision 11

Density at the token layer. One config. Eighty surfaces respond.

Finding Every grid started with hardcoded font and padding utilities — text-[13px], px-3, py-2 repeated across four data grids and dozens of cells. When users asked "can I fit more rows on screen," the only answer was a sprint editing every component.
Insight Density is the same architectural problem as dark mode. Bake it into components and you retrofit forever; bake it into tokens and one config controls every surface.
Design I built three configs — DENSITY, CARD_DENSITY, DETAIL_DENSITY — that emit CSS custom properties on the page wrapper. The user picks Compact / Comfortable / Spacious, and every grid cell, card tile, and detail section retightens in one paint with no per-component edits. Compact lands at twenty-pixel rows after iterating 8 → 12 → 16 → 20.
ADDITION 3 / 5 · Decision 12 — paste after Decision 11.
Decision 12

Bulk send and sequences ship as their own modules.

Finding My first pass buried bulk email and follow-up cadences in a kebab menu on the contact list. Recruiters at a staffing client I worked with never used the equivalent feature in their CRM because they couldn't find it.
Insight The work that earns a CRM its keep is outreach cadence, not record management. HubSpot, Outreach, and Apollo all promoted sequences to a top-level surface for the same reason.
Design I built Bulk Email and Sequences as their own pages. Bulk has its own stat row and a history feed with attachment previews; Sequences has a step funnel showing drop-off between touch one and touch three. Both render inside the same density tokens — flip Compact and the funnel tightens with everything else.

Part B — Issues in the existing Framer copy

Pulled from the screenshots you sent. Each item maps to a specific criterion. None of these block your additions — but they're cracks in the published case study that a hiring manager will land on inside the 8–46 second scan window.

ISSUE 1 · Privacy violation — Madison Resources named in public copy.
Section 7 of your criteria doc: "Madison client — Internal use only. Never name the actual client in public copy. When in doubt, use the industry or product type."

"Madison Resources" appears in at least six places in the published case study:

Hero, Problem two: "...the one every CRM I shipped fought chronically at Madison Resources recruiting and PayFactors compensation..."
Research / Career Inquiry header: "Dirty data was the problem at Madison Resources. Dirty data was the problem at PayFactors."
Research body: "Recruiters at Madison Resources spent hours every week reconciling duplicate candidate records..."
Decision 02 Insight: "Recruiters at Madison Resources asked me to remove this exact pattern years ago."
Decision 06 Insight: "support-ticket patterns at PayFactors and Madison Resources — tickets spiked in week three..."
Deliverable 03: "twelve years of shipped grid patterns from Madison Resources, PayFactors, and financial services..."

Suggested global replace: "Madison Resources" → "a national staffing platform" (or "a recruiting CRM I worked on"). Keep the rhetorical repetition pattern in the Research header by anonymizing both names if you want to play it safe — though PayFactors isn't on the privacy list so it can stay.

ISSUE 2 · Factual claim now overstated — refresh persistence.
Live demo section #3 promises something the app doesn't currently keep.
"3. Customize a grid — Open the Columns dropdown, pin a column, change density, save a view. Refresh — everything stays exactly as you left it."

Today's revert means column reorder, sort, filters, and saved views do not survive refresh. Only column widths, pinning, and density do. The current copy promises more than the app delivers — exactly the kind of detail a hiring manager who clicks the live demo will catch.

Suggested rewrite (narrows the claim):

3. Customize a grid — Open the Columns dropdown, pin a column, flip density. Refresh — your widths, pins, and density stay exactly as you left them.
